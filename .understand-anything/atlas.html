Research question
When a 4-bit quantized model looks “safer,” is that a real shift in its alignment — or just a less-capable model that fails to follow the harmful instruction?

This repository benchmarks five matched model pairs across four families (Qwen, Llama, Mistral, Phi, ≈1.7–7.2B). Each pair is the same model loaded two ways: a full-precision fp16 baseline versus on-the-fly NF4 4-bit quantization (with an INT8 precision point). Every model is measured on three axes:

HarmBench
Attack Success Rate — does it comply with harmful prompts? Scored by the official HarmBench 13B classifier (the judge), not a regex.
XSTest
Over-refusal rate — does it wrongly refuse benign prompts?
MMLU
Accuracy — the capability anchor, so a “safety” change can be separated from plain capability loss.

The headline, at the primary 512-token generation budget: after correcting for multiple comparisons (Benjamini–Hochberg FDR), no attack-success increase survives anywhere. The only statistically significant change in harmful compliance is Llama-3.2-3B going down ~0.04 under quantization. An earlier 128-token result that looked like a safety regression turned out to be a truncation artefact — the model was cut off mid-refusal. In short: the observed effects look like capability movement, not a genuine alignment shift.
