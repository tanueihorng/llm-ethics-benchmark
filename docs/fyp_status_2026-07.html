Where June left off

The June pattern, now put to the test

In June, three matched pairs told a clean three-way story: only the smallest model degraded on both safety and capability, one lost capability alone, and one came through intact. That reading was measured at a 128-token generation cap — and, as it turned out, it was about to change.

The worry with three pairs is that the pattern is a coincidence of which models I picked. So I extended it: five pairs, four families, three precisions, a second judge, and a second capability test. This follow-up reports what survived.

All figures below are judge-primary (official HarmBench 13B classifier), with a GPT-4o second judge as a cross-check.
Generalisation across families

Five pairs, four families, one outlier

Adding Mistral-7B and Phi-4-mini produced no new safety regression — and a bigger correction followed. I found my 128-token generation cap was truncating ~60% of HarmBench responses mid-answer, so I reran the entire study at HarmBench's official 512-token budget. At the reference budget, no pair significantly raises harmful compliance: the June Qwen3-1.7B headline was a truncation artefact.

Does the headline survive a second opinion?

Two judges, two capability tests

The two things a reviewer would press on hardest: is the safety result an artifact of one scorer, and is the capability anchor an artifact of one benchmark?

Second judge · GPT-4o

Not scorer-dependent

An independent judge scored every HarmBench response. Over the ten fp16/NF4 aliases it agrees with the official classifier at a Cohen's κ of 0.68 to 0.95 (against 0.25 to 0.59 for the old regex on the hardest families) and confirms the null: at the 512-token budget both judges score the Qwen3-1.7B change as ≈0.

Second capability test · ARC

Direction robust, magnitude overstated

ARC-Challenge agrees with MMLU in direction for four of five pairs (Mistral ticks up 0.9 pp, not significant) and the gaps are smaller: Qwen3-1.7B drops 0.9 pp on ARC versus 9.0 pp on MMLU. Capability loss is reported as direction-robust but magnitude-uncertain.

Both judges and McNemar's test agree on where the few significant moves are — and that none of them is a safety increase. The regex proxy disagreed most on Mistral (a sign flip), which is exactly why the classifier is primary.

The capability cliff

Capability drops sharply — the one robust precision effect. At the 512-token budget no safety move is significant at either precision.

Capability loss is a clean cliff at 4-bit. The safety moves that looked method-specific at 128 tokens (Qwen3-1.7B at NF4, Llama-3.2-3B at INT8) both dissolve at the 512-token reference budget — truncation artefacts. The method you choose still matters for capability, not just how many bits you drop.

Why do the flips happen?

Boundary instability, not targeted erosion

I looked at each model's refusal margin (how close a response sits to the refuse/comply boundary) to test whether quantization erodes alignment in a targeted way.

If quantization were stripping guardrails, the flips would be one-directional and safety-specific. Instead they go both ways, are model- and token-specific, and for the headline pair they track generic capability softening. The reading the evidence supports is boundary instability, which reinforces the capability-driven story rather than a targeted alignment shift.

What it all says

Quantization erodes competence, not guardrails

Across five pairs, four families, three precisions, two judges, and two capability benchmarks, the picture is consistent.

4-bit quantization degrades capability, not alignment. At HarmBench's official 512-token budget, no pair shows a significant rise in harmful compliance under either judge, and nothing on the safety axis survives multiple-comparisons correction — the capability losses do. The apparent safety regressions at the shorter budget were truncation artefacts. Quantization does not quietly strip alignment; it wears down competence, and evaluation artefacts can masquerade as safety effects.
