method

Descriptive, not evaluative

Every metric below is a simple, reproducible surface property computed over raw model outputs — medians and percentages, no judges, no scores. Open-ended metrics use the HarmBench + XSTest responses (450 per variant); format-discipline metrics use MMLU + ARC (1,472 per variant).

self-continuation

When the answer ends but the tokens don’t

Generation stops only at the end-of-sequence token. A chat-tuned model that finishes early keeps predicting “the conversation continues” — so it invents the next user turn and answers it. Turn markers are stripped in decoding, welding it into one paragraph.

generation budget

128 tokens made stubs; 512 made dialogues

At 128 tokens most models were cut mid-thought — the corpus behind the study’s 60.3% provable-truncation finding and the retired headline it dissolved. At 512, answers complete… and the leftover budget becomes invented conversation. Hallucinated self-dialogue is a budget phenomenon.

why it matters

Style explains the measurements

These personas are not trivia — they are the mechanism behind three of the study’s central findings.

Scoreability tracks terseness
Llama’s one-sentence discipline makes it the easiest model to score — the family where refusal-pattern and classifier scorers agree most. Mistral’s hedge-everything prose is exactly what breaks pattern-matching — the family where they agree least.
Truncation made the old headline
The 128-token stubs are what the scorers originally judged. Reading the raw transcripts — a human noticing the cut-offs — is what triggered the 512 rerun that dissolved the apparent safety regression.
Format erosion, not knowledge loss
NF4-Qwen’s bare-letter collapse shows quantization eroding answer formatting — measured here purely from style, independently confirming the report’s parser-bracket story.

One line for the viva: the five models differ as much in how they say things as in what they say — and scorer reliability tracks the former, not the latter.
