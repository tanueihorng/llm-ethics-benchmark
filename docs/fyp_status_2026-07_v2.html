Where June left off

The June pattern, now put to the test

In June, three matched pairs told a clean three-way story: only the smallest model degraded on both safety and capability, one lost capability alone, and one came through intact.

The worry with three pairs is that the pattern is a coincidence of which models I picked. So I extended it: five pairs, four families, three precisions, a second judge, and a second capability test. This follow-up reports what survived.

All figures below are judge-primary (official HarmBench 13B classifier), with a GPT-4o second judge as a cross-check.

Does the headline survive a second opinion?

Two judges, two capability tests

The two things a reviewer would press on hardest: is the safety result an artifact of one scorer, and is the capability anchor an artifact of one benchmark?

Second judge · GPT-4o

Not scorer-dependent

An independent judge scored every HarmBench response. It agrees with the official classifier at a Cohen's κ of 0.69 to 0.94 (against 0.19 to 0.37 for the old regex) and reproduces the Qwen3-1.7B rise in the same direction.

Second capability test · ARC

Direction robust, magnitude overstated

ARC-Challenge agrees with MMLU in direction (every pair loses capability) but the gaps are smaller: Qwen3-1.7B drops 1.3 pp on ARC versus 8.7 pp on MMLU. Capability loss is now reported as direction-robust but magnitude-uncertain.

Both judges and McNemar's test agree on the significant moves. The regex proxy disagreed most on Mistral (a sign flip), which is exactly why the classifier is primary.

Not a smooth dial

The capability cliff

Capability drops sharply. Qwen3-1.7B's safety rise lives here, not at INT8.

Capability loss is a clean cliff at 4-bit; the safety axis is method-specific (Qwen3-1.7B at NF4, Llama-3.2-3B at INT8). Which quantization method you choose matters in its own right, not just how many bits you drop.

Why do the flips happen?

Boundary instability, not targeted erosion

I looked at each model's refusal margin (how close a response sits to the refuse/comply boundary) to test whether quantization erodes alignment in a targeted way.

If quantization were stripping guardrails, the flips would be one-directional and safety-specific. Instead they go both ways, are model- and token-specific, and for the headline pair they track generic capability softening. The reading the evidence supports is boundary instability, which reinforces the capability-driven story rather than a targeted alignment shift.

What it all says

Quantization erodes competence, not guardrails

Across five pairs, four families, three precisions, two judges, and two capability benchmarks, the picture is consistent.

4-bit quantization mostly degrades capability. Only the smallest model, Qwen3-1.7B, shows a genuine and modest safety regression on top of that. Everywhere else, an apparent safety change is better explained by lost capability, or the model is simply robust. Quantization does not quietly strip alignment; it wears down competence, and the safety effects follow from that.
