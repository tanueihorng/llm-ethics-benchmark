01 The moment

Open source is having its moment.

Frontier models keep getting more expensive and more gated — and the response is a surge in open-weight and on-device AI. The centre of gravity is moving off the cloud and onto your own hardware.

In the news · the open & on-device surge
02 The shift

So capable AI is going local.

Open-weight models have all but caught the closed frontier, and 4-bit quantization shrinks them ~4× to run on a laptop or a single GPU. When DeepSeek's open R1 landed in January 2025, markets erased roughly $600B of NVIDIA's value in a day — a verdict that frontier-grade AI no longer needs the most expensive hardware.

03 The stakes

Which makes safety everyone's problem.

Once anyone can download, quantize and run — or modify — a capable model offline, its alignment is no longer the lab's responsibility. And alignment is fragile: it can be stripped for pocket change, and prior work shows the compression step itself can weaken a model's refusals.

fragile by fine-tuning

10 examples · <$0.20

enough to jailbreak a frontier model's safety guardrails — and even benign fine-tuning degraded alignment (Qi et al., 2023).

fragile by quantization

85% → 96%

Mistral-7B's jailbreak success rate after quantization in one study — precision cuts can weaken refusal (Kumar et al., 2024).

So: does the everyday 4-bit step itself change safety — or just capability? Prior work rarely separates the two. This project does.
Sources: Qi et al., 2023 (arXiv:2310.03693) · Kumar et al., 2024 (arXiv:2404.04392)
04 The research gap

A quieter model isn't always a safer one.

“Did the model get safer — or just get worse at everything, refusals included?”
the conflation prior work rarely separates
Hypothesis A · true alignment shift

The safety training itself moved.

Quantization perturbs the weights that encode refusal, genuinely shifting the ethical line — while capability on neutral tasks holds.

Hypothesis B · capability collapse

The model just got dumber.

Any "safety" change is a side-effect of broad degradation. A safety score is meaningless until anchored against what happened to capability.

05 The research question

Do safety changes in 4-bit models reflect a real alignment shift — or just capability degradation?

RQ1 · direction

Does 4-bit change harmful-prompt compliance, and which way?

RQ2 · over-refusal

Does it change refusals of perfectly benign requests?

RQ3 · capability

What is the simultaneous cost to capability — and does it explain the safety change?

RQ4 · mechanism

Where behavior moves, why — targeted erosion, or an unstable boundary?

RQ5 · precision

Is the effect graded by bit-width (fp16→INT8→NF4), or a cliff?

cross-cutting

Does any finding generalize across model families?

07 A turning point

My first scorer was measuring the wrong thing.

A regex refusal-matcher scored a response harmful whenever it failed to sound like a refusal — even when useless or incoherent. A 13B judge over the same outputs disagreed sharply.

10 Is it the bits?

The damage isn't graded by bit-width.

Tracing each pair across fp16 → INT8 → NF4: capability holds, holds, then drops off a cliff at 4-bit, while safety moves are model- and method-specific.

Safety across precision

The most judge-robust safety move is Llama-3B at INT8 (+4.0pp) — significant under both judges, but non-monotonic and small (~8 of 200 prompts). Suggestive, not settled.
11 The mechanism

Not erosion. Instability.

Probing each model's refusal margin: behavioral flips cluster where the baseline margin was already thin — but only weakly, and they go both ways. The quantized boundary jitters; it isn't selectively dismantled.

12 The answer

Mostly capability.
Rarely conscience.

Across five families and three precisions, 4-bit quantization did not broadly collapse alignment. The worrying corner — more harmful while just as capable — stayed empty: no pair lost safety without also losing capability. When a quantized model looks safer, it is usually because it got worse — not more principled.
